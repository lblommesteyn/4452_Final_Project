Automated Strong Gravitational Lens Detection
via Deep Learning in Large-Scale Sky Surveys

Virain Bawa
Dexter Luth

Project Proposal - CS 4452

Abstract

Strong gravitational lensing systems are rare but scientifically valuable phenomena that enable precise
measurements of dark matter distributions, galaxy mass profiles, and cosmological parameters. Manual
identification of lens candidates in modern sky surveys is increasingly infeasible because survey volume is
growing faster than expert review capacity, and the class imbalance is extreme: real lenses are vastly
outnumbered by ordinary galaxies and look-alike contaminants. This project develops and evaluates deep
learning models for automated strong lens detection using galaxy imaging data. We compare convolutional
neural networks (CNNs) against vision transformers (ViTs) under realistic imbalance settings, and we assess
both discrimination quality and probability calibration. Beyond standard classification metrics, we evaluate
precision-recall behavior at low false positive rates, measure robustness to image perturbations, and test
whether a human-in-the-loop (HITL) review stage improves performance on ambiguous candidates. The
overall goal is to characterize architectural trade-offs between modern transformer-based models and
established CNN baselines, and to determine whether calibrated automated ranking can support large-scale
survey pipelines such as Rubin/LSST-era lens searches.

1. Motivation and Problem Statement

Strong lenses are rare, but they are central to several astronomy and cosmology problems:

- mapping dark matter and total mass distributions in galaxies and clusters
- studying high-redshift source galaxies that are otherwise too faint to observe clearly
- constraining cosmological parameters through time-delay and population analyses

The core machine learning problem is therefore not only binary classification, but high-confidence ranking
under severe class imbalance. A useful survey model must produce a short, high-purity candidate list instead
of simply maximizing accuracy on a balanced benchmark. This project focuses on that operational setting.

2. Research Questions

- How does a ResNet-style CNN compare with a ViT on strong lens detection when both are trained on the
  same imaging data and evaluated under identical imbalance conditions?
- Which architecture yields better precision-recall performance at low false positive rates suitable for
  survey-scale candidate filtering?
- How well calibrated are the predicted probabilities, and can post-hoc temperature scaling improve their
  reliability?
- Does a simple HITL active learning loop improve performance on ambiguous or low-confidence examples?
- How robust are the trained models to realistic imaging perturbations such as noise, blur, contrast shifts,
  and small spatial misalignments?

3. Data Strategy

The original plan referenced the LSST Strong Lensing Science Collaboration, Galaxy Zoo, and Bologna Lens
Factory resources. After checking public access, the most practical public data plan is:

1. Rubin/LSST Strong Lensing Science Collaboration
   URL: https://sites.google.com/view/lsst-stronglensing
   Role: scientific context, survey requirements, and lens-finding task framing for Rubin-scale pipelines.

2. Strong Lens Challenge data release
   URL: https://slchallenge.cbpf.br/public/Strong_Lens_Challenge_Data_Release.pdf
   Role: primary public benchmark for simulated lens-finding experiments and baseline model comparison.

3. Galaxy Zoo data portal
   URL: https://data.galaxyzoo.org/index.html
   Role: large source of real non-lens galaxies and hard negatives for improving realism and reducing
   overfitting to simulated data.

4. Bologna Lens Factory challenge portal
   URL: http://metcalf1.difa.unibo.it/blf-portal/gg_challenge.html
   Role: secondary benchmark or backup positive set if the Strong Lens Challenge data are insufficient.

5. Optional external validation source: Space Warps and related survey lens candidate catalogs
   Role: small-scale qualitative and HITL-oriented validation on more realistic candidates if time permits.

Proposed dataset construction

- Positive class: simulated or curated lens examples from the Strong Lens Challenge and, if needed, Bologna
  Lens Factory.
- Negative class: non-lens galaxies from challenge negatives plus hard negatives from Galaxy Zoo.
- Validation strategy: maintain train, validation, and test splits with no image leakage, and evaluate both
  on a balanced benchmark split and on a more realistic imbalanced split such as 1:100 or 1:1000 lens
  prevalence.
- External realism check: reserve a small, separate set of real or candidate lenses for qualitative
  assessment of domain shift.

Preprocessing plan

- Convert all cutouts to a common image size, likely 96 x 96, 128 x 128, or 224 x 224 depending on model.
- Map available bands into a 3-channel image representation when possible.
- Apply pixel normalization using either min-max scaling or astrophysics-friendly intensity transforms such as
  log or asinh scaling.
- Use astronomy-safe augmentations: rotations, flips, small translations, blur, and noise injection.
- Track data provenance in a manifest so results can be stratified by source dataset.

4. Methods

4.1 Residual CNN Baseline

The first model is a ResNet-based CNN trained on lens and non-lens galaxy cutouts. The CNN serves as the
practical baseline because convolutional backbones remain strong on limited-data image classification tasks
and are cheaper to train than large transformers. We will begin with ResNet-18 or ResNet-34, then tune:

- learning rate and weight decay
- input resolution
- focal loss gamma
- class weighting and weighted sampling
- augmentation strength

4.2 Vision Transformer

The second model is a patch-based ViT applied to the same standardized cutouts. The ViT tests whether
global self-attention improves discrimination or candidate ranking in the presence of ring-like and arc-like
structures distributed across the image. Because transformers can be data-hungry, we will compare:

- training from scratch vs. transfer learning where feasible
- multiple input resolutions
- calibration quality relative to the CNN baseline
- robustness to perturbations and domain shift

4.3 Human-in-the-Loop Refinement

The third component is not a separate backbone but an iterative refinement loop. After an initial model is
trained, we will rank unlabeled or ambiguous examples by uncertainty or model disagreement. A human
reviewer then inspects only the highest-value candidates and either corrects labels or confirms predictions.
The updated labels are incorporated into another training round. This stage is intended to:

- reduce the impact of label noise
- improve recall on difficult borderline examples
- improve ranking quality for the final candidate list
- emulate a realistic survey workflow where experts review only a small filtered subset

5. Training Plan

The implementation will compare the following settings:

- Model family: ResNet vs. ViT
- Loss function: weighted binary cross-entropy vs. focal loss
- Sampling strategy: natural class ratio vs. weighted sampler
- Calibration: raw logits vs. temperature-scaled probabilities
- HITL: no review loop vs. one or more targeted review rounds

Training details

- Optimizer: AdamW
- Early stopping on validation average precision
- Best-model checkpointing
- Class imbalance handling with weighted sampling and positive-class weighting
- Repeatable random seeds and fixed manifest splits for fair comparison

6. Evaluation Protocol

The main evaluation goal is not only to classify correctly, but to identify a threshold and ranking strategy
that is usable in a real survey screening pipeline.

Primary metrics

- AUC-ROC
- Average precision / area under the precision-recall curve
- Precision, recall, and F1 at a fixed operating threshold
- Precision at very low false positive rates such as 1e-2 and 1e-3
- Recall at top-K ranked candidates

Calibration metrics

- Expected calibration error (ECE)
- Brier score
- Reliability diagrams
- Improvement after temperature scaling

Robustness tests

- additive Gaussian noise
- blur and PSF-like smoothing
- brightness and contrast changes
- small translations and rotations
- partial band corruption or low signal-to-noise conditions

Ablation plan

- Run a small ablation set covering ResNet vs. ViT, raw vs. calibrated probabilities, and at least one imbalance/sampler/loss comparison.
- compare performance with and without hard negatives from Galaxy Zoo
- compare balanced vs. survey-like class prevalence during evaluation
- compare raw and calibrated probabilities
- compare single-model uncertainty vs. CNN/ViT disagreement for HITL queue construction

7. Expected Outcomes

We expect the project to produce:

- a reproducible training pipeline for binary lens detection
- a baseline comparison between ResNet and ViT backbones
- calibrated probability estimates for candidate ranking
- an uncertainty-based HITL review queue for ambiguous examples
- a short discussion of the simulation-to-reality gap and how it affects deployment

Hypotheses

- The ResNet baseline will likely be more stable and efficient on limited training data.
- The ViT may improve global pattern modeling, but only if enough data or transfer learning is available.
- Calibration will matter significantly because survey deployment depends on ranking and thresholding rather
  than raw class labels alone.
- HITL refinement will improve precision on difficult examples without requiring full manual review.

8. Risks and Mitigation

Risk: Domain shift between simulated lenses and real survey images.
Mitigation: Mix simulated positives with real non-lens negatives, report source-specific metrics, and include
an external validation set where possible.

Risk: Extreme class imbalance makes naive accuracy misleading.
Mitigation: Emphasize precision-recall metrics, low-FPR operating points, and calibrated ranking.

Risk: Limited compute budget for transformer experiments.
Mitigation: Use small backbones first, keep image sizes modest, and prioritize one well-tuned ViT baseline
over many under-trained variants.

Risk: Label noise or ambiguous negatives.
Mitigation: Use HITL review rounds, uncertainty sampling, and careful error analysis of false positives.
